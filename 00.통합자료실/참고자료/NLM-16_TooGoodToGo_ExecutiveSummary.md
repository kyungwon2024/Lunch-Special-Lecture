Executive Summary 

 The newly proposed Too Good To Go (TGTG) app plugin targets SDG 12.3, which seeks to reduce worldwide food waste by 50% by 2030 (United Nations 2017). The new feature involves real-time data, artificial intelligence, and the Internet of Things (IoT). It will provide new cutting-edge technology, and the novel plugin will enhance food waste management and may revolutionise food waste control using predictive analysis. This is opposed to applying a reactive approach, which has been done until today. This plugin enables individuals and organisations to avoid food waste by offering immediate insights using AI-driven analytics and IoT-enabled food storage monitoring. The novel plugin method shows the potential to dramatically reduce global food waste, thus aiding environmental and economic sustainability.  Identification of the Problem WWF (2021) states that approximately 1.3 billion tons of food are wasted globally annually, which accounts for one-third of the total food production. The accumulation of waste leads to higher greenhouse gas emissions, incurs financial costs, and hinders food delivery (Amicarelli, Lagioia, and Bux 2021). Many existing solutions depend on the transfer of excess food, which only targets waste after the fact. This application supports users in creating less wastage; the plugin offers real-time food consumption data and predictive analytics. This tech-enabled strategy advances Sustainable Development Goal 12.3 (United Nations 2017), which aims to address food waste at its origin.  Strategic Targeting and Onboarding The intended audience of this plugin is environmentally aware customers, small-to-medium enterprises, and sustainability groups. Environmentally responsible consumers, such as urban professionals aged 25-45, who seek food tracking and management solutions are targeted. For restaurant owners, inventory management and waste reduction are beneficial. Thus, small-to-medium-sized enterprises, specifically those in the food service industry, enable cost savings and improve operational efficiency. The data generated by the plugin may guide environmental campaigns and initiatives undertaken by climate-conscious organisations, such as non-governmental organisations (NGOs).  According to Rogers’ (1962) diffusion theory, potential early adopters are likely to be technologically adept consumers knowledgeable about sustainability applications and organisations aiming to enhance eco-friendliness and reduce expenses. Additionally, the plugin's artificial intelligence and Internet of Things (IoT) applications may encourage early adopters by streamlining a challenging food control task. The introduction approach will take place via video tutorials, educational programs, and environmentally sustainable business partnerships to further foster acceptance. Observations and feedback from early adopters will be gathered to broaden its acceptance.  The plugin achieves its goal by reaching sub target SDG 12.3.1 - to transform food waste. Implementing this this data-driven and real-time plugin can improve environmental, economic, and societal issues. Eco-friendly consumers, businesses, and NGOs are strategically targeted to ensure the plugin has the potential to swiftly gain traction and become a vital tool in the global battle to reduce food waste. Embedding of AI and IoT technology in food waste management provides a competitive advantage and initiates a new sustainability standard.

Concept Diagram 

 1. In the first part, IoT sensors track real-time food storage conditions such as temperature and humidity. The sensors alert involved organisations to look after their perishable goods. Thus, food freshness and less waste from inadequate storage can be assured. 2. The second part integrates AI and machine learning algorithms to identify consumption patterns and predict trends. The AI integration supports companies in managing food stock and constantly evaluates data, which leads to improved supply-demand stability. 3. Thirdly, the software forecasts surplus food to prevent waste through predictive analytics. The system provides real-time forecasts using sales data, consumer demand, and expiration dates to assist companies in managing inventory and preventing overproduction. 4. The fourth and last part implements the user interfaces, providing a user-friendly dashboard for businesses and individuals, with real-time inventory, food storage, and waste forecasts for businesses. The surplus food display enables a quick and informed choice of affordable products before they go to waste. The design promotes easy user interaction, enhancing the plugin's effectiveness.

Triple Bottom Line Analysis 

 The TGTG food waste-reducing app connects consumers and businesses to save surplus food. The following analysis uses the Elkington and James (1997) triple bottom line framework to provide a thorough overview of the environmental, social, and economic factors while examining the app's new real-time plugin to highlight the advantages, risks, and mitigation strategies.  Environmental Dimension Benefits and significance: The TGTG plugin aims to reduce food waste significantly by offering forecasting analytics to assist organisations in managing inventory better to minimise surplus food. Success will be shown through lower food waste, reduced carbon emissions, and increased consumer awareness of environmental effects. The integrated sensor data is used in the plugin to improve tracking of food storage conditions and to control spoilage rates.  Negative impact and risks: Through the efficiency of showing data in real-time, it could unintentionally create new transportation emissions, as users may travel further for food pickups. As restaurants and small to medium businesses get used to applying the app, they may start overproduction, as businesses think surplus will always sell through the app. Thus, TGTG should promote eco-friendly practices and encourage local pickup options to reduce risks.  Social Dimension Benefits and significance: The TGTG app plugin promotes social equity by offering budget-friendly food options, supporting affordable food choices in underserved areas, aiding local businesses, and encouraging community involvement. TGTG supports local businesses, thereby strengthening community ties. The app measures social impact and reflects this on its display, which may support responsible consumer behaviour. User involvement in local businesses and diversity will serve as indicators of progress.  Negative impact and risks: There is a worry that discounted food could be misused, with some people buying in bulk for resale instead of personal consumption. In specific demographics in Australia, access to smartphones or the internet may pose technological limitations. Therefore, TGTG should impose buying restrictions and work with community organisations for broader access.  Economic Dimension Benefits and significance: The TGTG plugin economically supports businesses in recovering costs from surplus food while reducing food waste disposal expenses. Furthermore, the TGTG network may create new jobs in the food industry, incorporating roles and positions associated with delivery, food preparation, and app management. Indicators of successful implementation are recently acquired organisations or businesses, sales revenue from surplus meals sold and reduced disposal costs.  Negative impact and risks: The app may encourage businesses to surplus food while relying on the platform to offload it. On this matter, TGTG should advocate sustainable food production methods. Furthermore, it should limit the overuse of the app for leftover food management.  

 

Legal and Ethical Implications 

 The introduction of a new plugin for the TGTG app entails significant legal ramifications, particularly with personal data, privacy, and equity in food waste management. The plugin must appropriately address such concerns by integrating predictive analytics, Artificial Intelligence (AI), and Internet of Things (IoT) technology to guarantee alignment with current legislation and uphold ethical integrity.  Legal Implications Regulatory frameworks like the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) and the Australian Privacy Principles (APP) regulate the collection and use of personal data in Europe and Australia, respectively. The TGTG plugin's user data gathering includes personal preferences, location, and possibly complex usage patterns, all requiring compliance with these requirements. The General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) mandates that companies collect only necessary data and secure informed consent from consumers (European Union 2016). Moreover, the app requires that organisations demonstrate transparency in their data practices and protect user privacy under the oversight of the Office of the Australian Information Commissioner (OAIC) (OAIC 2018). Failure to adhere to these regulations may result in significant legal penalties and damage the application's reputation.  Moreover, the Terms and Conditions of Use must specify the purpose of data collection, the methods of data processing, and the users’ rights over their data (Taal 2021). Thus, this comprises the ability to refuse some data collection methods, access their data, and request its deletion. This degree of legal clarity mitigates liability and fosters user confidence.  Ethical Implications Ethical considerations impact the creation and implementation of the TGTG plugin. Equity, justice, and autonomy should be prioritised in the design and functionality of plugins. Users must provide personal information to use the plugin, infringing on autonomy. User data must be safeguarded against manipulation that takes advantage of consumption patterns without consent (Bu et al. 2020).  The plugin must be implemented with equity and justice. The technology must be available to all users, particularly small firms and economically disadvantaged communities that cannot afford contemporary trash management solutions. Ethical design entails that the plugin prevents the creation of disparities between larger, affluent organisations that can fully utilise the technology and smaller enterprises that may find it challenging to adopt it (Floridi 2023a, 2023b). Beneficence is crucial as the tool must mitigate food waste and encourage sustainable consumption among individuals and organisations.  The plugin presents significant privacy and security issues; see OAIC’s (2018) Guide. Using IoT sensors and AI algorithms to monitor user activity heightens the risk of data breaches, hacking, and unauthorised access to sensitive information. To safeguard users' personal and financial information, data encryption, two-factor authentication, and secure payment systems are essential (Yee 2012). Security breaches contravene privacy regulations and undermine trust, crucial for user engagement with plugins.  The legal and ethical implications of the TGTG plugin are substantial and necessitate meticulous attention to data protection, regulatory adherence, and equitable benefit allocation. The plugin will offer sustainable food waste management solutions while safeguarding users' rights by adhering to autonomy, beneficence, and justice principles and complying with GDPR and app regulations.
