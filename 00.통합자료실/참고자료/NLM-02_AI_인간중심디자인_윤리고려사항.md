Conversation: AI in human-centered design: Ethical considerations 

Brendan Mapes*, Judy Park Lee 

Public Policy Lab, United States of America 

*Corresponding e-mail: bmapes@publicpolicylab.org 

doi.org/10.21606/drs.2024.1660 

Abstract: Artificial intelligence offers designers ample opportunities to innovate and enhance their work. But before incorporating AI tools for design research in the real world, we must think carefully about the risks that doing so may pose. In this session, we discussed how to innovate with AI in a safe, ethical, and responsible way. We explored potential applications of AI in human-centered design research and established a framework for weighing and mitigating the risks of AI’s use. The session set the table for an ongoing conversation within the design research community about how to leverage the power of AI in our work while minimizing the potential for harm to the humans we design for. 

Keywords: artificial intelligence; human-centered design; ethics  

1. Context 

1.1 Motivation  Designers have quickly embraced AI to innovate and improve their work. They’ve been swift 

to incorporate generative AI tools into their practice. Of 1,000 designers and creatives polled 

in 2023 by Adobe, 81% had already started using generative AI in their work. Tools like 

Adobe Firefly have already shown promise in producing work like that of entry-level 

designers [1].  

While AI’s power and potential in design research are hard to ignore, the risks of using of AI 

in design research are equally apparent. For example, when left unchecked and unregulated, 

AI can reinforce systemic inequalities rooted in racism, sexism, and other harmful biases. As 

design researchers, we conduct research directly with human participants. By deploying AI 

tools in our work, we expose those participants and their data to any harm that AI may 

cause. Therefore, we must carefully consider and weigh the various risks of AI, including the 

reinforcement of systemic bias, prior to deploying AI tools.  

 

 

2 

 

There are also fundamental differences between design researchers’ traditional approach to 

research and the way in which AI tools function, which are equally important to consider. In 

design research we embrace messy data and dig into the nuances of users’ experiences. We 

seek out “thick data” because it’s the most powerful fuel for our design work, [2]. AI does 

the opposite. Instead, it looks for patterns and aims to generalize. It’s fueled by “big data,” 

or data that lives on the surface. 

In this conversation session, we created a space for practitioners to discuss risks associated 

with AI’s use and explore the tension between AI’s approach and human-centered design’s 

approach to analyzing and synthesizing data. Through these reflections, we aimed to support 

attendees in incorporating AI into their practice in a way that’s safe and responsible, while 

still innovative.  

1.2 Guiding questions  The following questions guided our conversation. The primary, overarching research 

question was as follows:   

As design researchers, how can we ensure our use of AI tools is safe, ethical, 

and responsible? 

That question called for engagement with the following sub-questions: 

To use AI safely and responsibly in a human-centered design research context, 

what questions must we ask ourselves before deploying those tools?  

What are the unique risks that AI poses when used for design research, and in 

which contexts must we be especially cautious?  

How do we properly inform research participants about our use of AI tools and 

give them more agency in deciding when and how they are used? 

Once we've decided that the use of AI tools is safe and responsible for a given 

design research project, how do we ensure that those tools continue to be used 

safely? How do we monitor and evaluate the safety of AI tools over the long 

term?

exploring how to leverage generative artificial intelligence and other types of machine

1.4 Conversation setup  The session was held in a hybrid format, with attendees participating both in-person and 

virtually. To begin the conversation, we presented a short introduction to AI and a 

framework from which to consider the ethics of using AI technology.  

After presentation of this background information, we introduced a case study to be 

discussed in small group discussions. In groups of 5-10, attendees discussed the example 

application of AI in human-centered design research, as well as other examples of AI used in 

their own work. Attendees were provided with a handout listing some of the key 

considerations for ethical AI, as presented by the facilitators in their introductory 

presentation. Each group assigned a notetaker to document highlights from the 

conversation.  

After 30 minutes of discussion in small groups, the larger group reconvened, and each group 

shared the highlights from their conversation. To conclude, we summarized the key 

takeaways from each group’s conversation and answered questions from attendees. 

1.5 Conversation attendees   An estimated 40 attendees participated in the session. During a pre-session live poll (n=29), 

attendees rated their frequency of use of AI tools from a scale of 1 (never using AI tools) to 5 

(using AI tools all the time), resulting in an average of 3.2. The most frequently cited 

application of AI tools was planning for research (16 votes), followed by synthesizing 

research (12 votes), and conducting research (11 votes). From these results, and our 

discussions, we inferred that many of the attendees had prior experience with both design 

research methods and applying AI tools to the research process.

2.1 Conceptual foundations We first defined terms to be used throughout the session, to ensure all attendees shared a 

baseline knowledge of and common language for topics to be discussed throughout the 

session. Presented definitions included the following:  

Design research: The systemic process of discovering new knowledge in order to 

design something in the world.  

Artificial intelligence: Technology that allows computers and machines to mimic 

human-level intelligence, including algorithms for simple tasks like regression 

and classification, or more complex algorithms like neural networks.  [2] 

Large language models (LLMs): A type of machine learning model that generates 

language as its output. LLMs utilize deep learning, a type of machine learning 

built with neural networks, which process data in a way that’s inspired by the 

human brain. [3] 

AI ethics: The field of study exploring how to optimize AI’s positive impact while 

reducing risks and avoiding adverse outcomes. [4]

After outlining those definitions and an overview of potential applications of AI in design 

research, we introduced a framework for considering the ethical implications of any use of 

AI, specifically calling upon The Belmont Report’s three basic principles for ethical human 

subject research: 1) respect for persons, 2) beneficence, and 3) justice [5]. We translated 

these three principles into three related criteria that provided a foundation for critical 

evaluation of AI tools throughout the conversation:  

Consent: Respecting the individual’s autonomy and obtaining informed consent 

for use of their data with some AI technology.  

Harm mitigation: Minimizing potential harms while maximizing potential 

benefits.  

Equity: Distributing the risks and benefits of research equitably.

3. Reflection  

3.1 Reflection 1: On the reinforcement of bias AI’s reinforcement of bias was an extensive topic of discussion. Attendees were aware of AI’s 

potential to reproduce or reinforce harmful biases, and as they brainstormed ways to 

 

 

11 

 

mitigate this risk, they reached an interesting conclusion: AI tools are no more likely to 

produce or reinforce harmful biases than humans themselves.  

Put another way, the bias we notice in the outputs of AI tools comes as direct result of bias 

existing in the data used to train those AI models, which is produced by humans. AI’s bias is a 

reflection of our own bias. Therefore, as we use AI we must be aware of the bias or 

imbalances existing in the training data for AI tools and work to counteract the negative 

effects of imbalances in that data — but we must recognize and contend with the reality 

that this bias is unavoidable.  

With this in mind, attendees stressed that researchers should maintain visibility and control 

over any AI tool’s output, so we can exercise our own judgment and professional expertise in 

determining if what has been created reinforces harmful biases for the populations we work 

with. One apt analogy for how we should consider AI’s role is to think of these tools as a 

novice research assistant or student driver, who requires a lot of close supervision.   

Relatedly, to combat these biases, attendees noted that there is a strong need to create 

more inclusive data sets representing the lived experiences of underrepresented 

populations. At PPL, we’ve been working on this challenge through projects like The People 

Say, an online research database (www.thepeoplesay.org) that captures rich qualitative data 

from a diverse cross-section of older Americans and caregivers across the country. That 

project focuses on the experiences of communities often under-consulted in policymaking, 

including older adults of color, those who are low income, and/or those who live in rural 

areas where healthcare isn’t easily accessible. Projects like this, aimed at addressing 

imbalances in publicly available data potentially used to train AI tools, can help combat the 

negative effects of bias when AI tools are used in design research.  

3.2 Reflection 2: On the role of factual information in design research Another common concern discussed by attendees was AI’s apparent tendency to produce 

misinformation. The group was reluctant to endorse applications of AI if its outputs couldn’t 

be validated, citing examples of AI producing misinformation as a reason for concern. The 

group acknowledged that AI tools like ChatGPT are not meant to report facts – they’re 

designed to identify patterns. The group discussed what that may mean for potential 

applications of AI in design research.  

Attendees noticed something similar in traditional methods of design research that don’t 

incorporate AI. Traditional design research isn’t after facts or objective truths either. Instead, 

the research simply aims to understand users’ experience with products and services. 

Consequently, the findings of that research can often be quite subjective – but that’s not 

problematic. As one participant stated, “Facts might not be the most important [element] in 

a design process.” 

What we’re after as design researchers is inspiration for our design work. Our research aims 

to be generative, not evaluative. We don’t aim to ascertain facts or confirm truths about the 

 

 

12 

 

world. We’re simply looking for things that will inspire our design and that will help us to be 

creative.  

Attendees concluded that when using AI in design it should be used in pursuit of that goal – 

to provide inspiration for design work, not uncover objective truths about the world. The 

group felt that if it were used in that way, AI’s use in design research would be safe, 

responsible, and ethical.
